Inequality of Women in Brazil

Understanding the unequal economic, social, and political conditions that contribute to the vulnerability of women and girls across Brazil.

Economic Inequality

Women in Brazil face persistent economic disparities, including unequal pay, limited access to high-earning jobs, and disproportionate responsibility for unpaid caregiving labor. These factors increase financial dependence and vulnerability.

Education Barriers

Although schooling is more accessible in Brazil, girls—especially in rural and low-income areas—drop out more often. Reasons include household chores, helping earn money, and teen pregnancy. Families sometimes prioritize boys’ education, and girls often take on extra household chores, creating additional barriers. These challenges make it harder for girls to stay in school and reach their full potential.

Racial & Regional Inequality

Racial and regional inequalities in Brazil create deep disparities. Black and Brown Brazilians earn less and face more discrimination, while the North and Northeast lag behind the wealthier South and Southeast in education, health, and income. These gaps overlap, leaving Black and Indigenous communities particularly vulnerable, and despite policies like affirmative action and Bolsa Família, income disparities persist.

Political Underrepresentation

"Despite accounting for 52 percent of the population, women represent only 15 percent of legislators and 11 percent of ministers in Brazil." The Wilson Center argues that, nearly 90 years after women won the right to vote, political empowerment remains very limited. Brazil’s ranking on the Global Gender Gap’s Political Empowerment index has dropped significantly in recent years. Progress in representation has been slow due to institutional, economic, and cultural barriers. At the current pace, the article estimates it could take 145 years for Brazil to reach gender parity in political power.

How Inequality Leads to Violence (SDG 5.2)

These inequalities—economic, educational, racial, and political—directly contribute to women's vulnerability to violence, exploitation, and trafficking. Limited access to resources and protection increases risk and decreases the ability to escape abusive situations.
